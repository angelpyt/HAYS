Youth Tobacco Survey Analysis¶

In this resource, we provide supporting data, graphs, and evidence of important trends in youth tobacco usage to get attention from parents, schools, organizations, and the government.

The dataset we are using is the Youth Tobacco Survey (YTS) Data, which has been conducted by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) yearly since 1998. The dataset we accessed through Data.gov, where CDC has published and made it publicly accessible, includes only data for years between 1999 to 2015.

This resource provides information on:

  • The overall trend of youth tobacco usage from 1999 to 2015.
  • How the youth smoking users and trends differ in different states in the U.S.
  • How the smoking status differs between males and females.
  • How the percentage of current smokers who want to quit varies throughout the years

Current Smoker Analysis¶

In this section we analyze current smoker trends for youth between 1999-2015. We focus on gender and education as two primary factors in our analysis. We also look at geographic location but decided that it isn't an important factor to continue with moving forward.

Current Youth Smoker Percentage by Year and Gender¶

First we graph the mean overall percentage of current smokers. All states and education are combined and it is split between Male and Female self-identified gender. Here we can clearly see the overall decreasing trend over time for both males and females.

Tobacco Users Who Attempt to Quit¶

Then we graph the average percentage of total youth tobacco users who actually attempt to quit by education level for each year. There is an unusual spike in percentage of quit attempts in 2012 for both middle and high schoolers, although the middle school tobacco users had a much greater sudden increase.

It is interesting then, that the average percentage of users who attempt to quit continues to stay peaked while the percentage of users who want to quit decreases. This could potentially be explained by a past attempt to quit, which led to decreased current interest in quitting.

Linear model Fitting Current Smokers Throughout the Past Decade¶

We fit a linear model for current smokers through the past decade. The adjusted R-squared for current smokers is 0.759, indicating good fit of the model to the data. Our P-values are also significant between current smoking rate and males and between current smoking rate and middle school. The coefficient for males is positive while it is negative for middle school education. This indicates that there is a significant positive relationship between males and currently smoking status, and a significant negative relationship between middle schoolers and current smoking.
